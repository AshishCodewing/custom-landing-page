THE JOURNEY

Your 15-Day Adventure — Day by Day

From the moment you land in Kathmandu to the moment you stand at 5,364m Everest Base Camp, every detail is handled. Read through it. Picture yourself there. Then ask us anything you want to know.

01

Arrival in Kathmandu

Kathmandu · 1,350m · Hotel · Welcome Dinner

You land in Kathmandu — a city that hits you with color, sound, and energy the moment you step outside the airport. Our representative will be waiting with your name on a sign at Tribhuvan International Airport and will transfer you directly to your hotel in Thamel, Kathmandu's vibrant hub for travelers and trekkers.

This evening, you will meet your Sherpa guide and fellow trekkers over a welcome dinner. Your guide will walk you through the coming 14 days, answer every question you have, and do a quick check of your gear to make sure you have everything you need.

Tonight, rest well. The adventure begins tomorrow.

02

Fly Kathmandu to Lukla — Trek to Phakding

Phakding · 2,610m · 4 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

An early morning drive to the domestic terminal for your flight to Lukla at 2,840m. The flight itself is one of the most dramatic short flights in the world — the runway at Tenzing-Hillary Airport sits on a mountainside, is 527 meters long, and ends at a cliff edge. Most trekkers land in Lukla with wide eyes and a new definition of what an airport can be.

From Lukla, your trek begins immediately. You walk through pine and rhododendron forests, cross your first suspension bridge over the rushing Dudh Koshi River, and descend gently through small Sherpa villages to the town of Phakding.

This is a manageable first day by design. Your body needs to find its trail legs. Tonight in Phakding, you eat your first teahouse meal and sleep to the sound of a river you will cross again tomorrow. The Himalayas have begun.

03

Phakding to Namche Bazaar

Namche Bazaar · 3,440m · 6 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Today is the day many trekkers first feel the mountains in their legs — and fall completely in love with this trail.

You cross the famous Hillary Suspension Bridge — 60 meters above the Dudh Koshi River, 96 meters long, with views straight down into white water and straight up to snow-capped peaks. There will be yaks crossing this bridge. There will be prayer flags. There will be a moment where you stop and look and understand that you are genuinely somewhere remarkable.

You enter Sagarmatha National Park and begin the long, steady ascent to Namche Bazaar — a climb that reveals the town gradually, tucked into a natural horseshoe bowl in the mountainside. Namche is the capital of the Khumbu region. It has bakeries, coffee shops, gear stores, and a Saturday market that draws traders from across the valley.

Tonight you sleep at 3,440 meters. Your body is beginning to notice the altitude. That is normal. That is expected. Your guide is watching.

04
Acclimatization

Acclimatization Day — Namche Bazaar

Namche Bazaar · 3,440m · Rest + Optional Hike · Teahouse · All Meals

This day is not optional and it is not wasted time. Acclimatization is the process your body uses to adapt to lower oxygen levels at altitude. It cannot be rushed. It cannot be skipped. Every serious EBC itinerary includes it — the ones that skip it have significantly higher rates of trekkers failing to reach Base Camp.

Today we follow the principle that high-altitude medicine has confirmed for decades: climb high, sleep low. You will hike up to the Everest View Hotel at approximately 3,880m — high enough to challenge your body — and return to Namche to sleep at 3,440m. This cycle triggers your body to produce additional red blood cells, improving oxygen delivery for the days ahead.

The optional morning hike takes you to a ridgeline with your first unobstructed, full-face view of Everest. No clouds between you and the summit. Just you, your guide, and the world's tallest mountain across a valley. Optional: hike to Khumjung Village and the school Edmund Hillary built.

Afternoon is yours. Walk the Namche market. Drink coffee at a Himalayan cafe. Rest. Your body is doing important work today, even when you are sitting still.

05

Namche Bazaar to Tengboche

Tengboche · 3,860m · 5 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

The trail from Namche to Tengboche is one of the most visually stunning days of the entire trek. You contour along the valley wall with unobstructed views of Ama Dablam — a peak so elegant and dramatic that many mountaineers consider it the most beautiful mountain in the world. You will photograph it repeatedly. The photographs will not do it justice.

You descend to Phunki Thanka, cross the river, and begin the ascent to Tengboche. The monastery appears above the treeline as you climb — the largest monastery in the Khumbu region, sitting at 3,860m with Everest directly behind it.

If you arrive before 5pm you may be able to sit quietly at the back of the monastery for the evening puja — the Buddhist prayer ceremony that has been performed here every evening for generations. Your guide will let you know.

Tengboche changes something in most trekkers. The combination of the altitude, the monastery, the views, and the growing understanding that you are genuinely deep in the Himalaya — it shifts something internally. Most people feel it. Very few have the words for it.

06

Tengboche to Dingboche

Dingboche · 4,410m · 5 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Today you cross 4,000 meters for the first time.

The trail takes you through Pangboche — one of the oldest Sherpa villages in the Khumbu, with apple orchards, stone walls built centuries ago, and a small monastery that pre-dates Tengboche. Your guide may have family here. More than once, a trekker with Nepal Social Treks has stopped at a teahouse in Pangboche and shared tea with the guide's grandmother.

The valley opens as you climb and the peaks around you grow more dramatic. Lhotse — the world's fourth highest mountain — fills the horizon directly ahead. Island Peak appears to your left. Makalu, the world's fifth highest, is visible in the distance.

You arrive in Dingboche at 4,410m. The air is noticeably thinner now. You may feel it on the stairs of the teahouse. You may sleep less deeply than usual. Your guide checks your oxygen levels this evening. Everyone's numbers are noted.

07
Acclimatization

Acclimatization Day — Dingboche

Dingboche · 4,410m · Rest + Optional Hike to 5,100m · Teahouse · All Meals

Your second acclimatization day — and the one that matters most for reaching Base Camp successfully.

At 4,410m, your body is working significantly harder than at sea level just to perform basic functions. Altitude affects appetite, sleep, mood, and energy. All of this is normal. This acclimatization day exists specifically to give your body time to produce more red blood cells and adapt before the final high-altitude push.

The optional hike today climbs to Nagarjun Hill at approximately 5,100m — higher than anything in the continental United States. The summit offers a 360-degree panorama of eight-thousanders that stops most trekkers completely still: Makalu, Lhotse, Baruntse, Cho Oyu, Island Peak. The scale of what surrounds you becomes impossible to deny.

Your guide monitors oxygen levels twice today — morning and evening. Rest this afternoon. Eat as much as your altitude-suppressed appetite allows. Drink more water than feels necessary. Tomorrow you push toward Base Camp.

08

Dingboche to Lobuche

Lobuche · 4,910m · 4.5 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Lobuche sits at 4,910m. Today is the day the trek begins to feel genuinely high.

The trail takes you past the Thukla Memorial — a ridge covered in stone chortens and cairns for climbers lost on Everest. Names and dates carved into stone. Photographs weathered by altitude wind. It is a quietly profound place that most trekkers spend longer at than they expected to. Your guide knows many of the stories behind the memorials.

The landscape changes above Thukla. Vegetation thins. Valleys broaden. You are walking through glacial moraine now — rock and rubble deposited by glaciers over thousands of years. The scale makes you feel small in a way that feels good rather than frightening.

Lobuche tonight. Your last full night before Base Camp. Many trekkers report that sleep is difficult at this altitude — the air is thin enough that your body occasionally wakes itself to breathe more deeply. This is called Cheyne-Stokes breathing and it is normal at altitude. Your guide will have mentioned this before now. Tomorrow is the day.

09
The Big Day

Lobuche to Gorak Shep — Excursion to Everest Base Camp

Gorak Shep · 5,140m · EBC 5,364m · 7 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

THE MOMENT EVERYTHING HAS BEEN BUILDING TOWARD

You wake before 6am. Breakfast is quick. The air outside the teahouse is cold and still and perfectly clear. Your guide checks oxygen levels. You shoulder your daypack — your porter carries the main bags to Gorak Shep — and you begin walking.

Trek from Lobuche to Gorak Shep (5,140m), drop your bags, and continue toward Base Camp. The trail runs along the lateral moraine of the Khumbu Glacier — rocky, uneven, and demanding. At 5,000m your body is producing roughly 40% less energy from each breath than at sea level. You walk more slowly than you have ever walked in your life. You stop more often. That is exactly right.

Then the prayer flags appear. Strung across the rocks at the end of the moraine, in every color, snapping in the Himalayan wind. Beyond them, the Khumbu Icefall towers above — the chaotic, beautiful river of ice that expedition climbers navigate on their way to the summit.

You are at Everest Base Camp. 5,364 meters. 17,598 feet above sea level.

Stand there for a moment. Look at the mountain above you. Feel what you feel. Every early morning, every steep section, every altitude headache you pushed through brought you here. Return to Gorak Shep for the night. Tomorrow you go even higher.

10
Kala Patthar Sunrise

Kala Patthar Sunrise — Trek to Pheriche

Pheriche · 4,270m · Kala Patthar 5,643m · 8 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Your alarm goes off at 3:30am. It is dark. It is cold. And this is the morning most trekkers remember above all others.

Kala Patthar — translated as "Black Rock" — is a subsidiary peak of Pumori that sits at 5,643m above Gorak Shep. It is not technically difficult to reach but it demands everything you have at this altitude in pre-dawn darkness. You reach the summit as the sky begins to lighten behind the Himalayan ridgeline.

And then, across the glacial valley below you, Everest appears in the first light of morning. The south face. The summit pyramid catching the sun before it touches anything else on earth. The photograph you have seen on posters and magazine covers and Instagram feeds for years — that photograph is taken from exactly where you are standing.

Take as long as you need. Your guide will wait.

Then you begin the long descent — all the way from Kala Patthar through Lobuche and Thukla down to Pheriche at 4,270m. Today is physically demanding but emotionally unforgettable. By the time you reach your teahouse, you have started at 3:30am, reached 5,643m, and walked further than any other day on the trek. Your appetite returns at altitude. Most trekkers eat everything on the menu tonight.

11

Pheriche to Tengboche

Tengboche · 3,860m · 5 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

The descent continues and with every hundred meters of altitude lost, something lifts. Energy returns. Conversation picks up. Appetites return to normal. Headaches that you barely noticed on the way up are suddenly, noticeably, gloriously absent.

You walk back through villages you passed on the ascent. They look different now. You look at them differently now. You are a person who has stood at Everest Base Camp and Kala Patthar. Everything you see on the way down is seen through different eyes.

Tengboche welcomes you back. The monastery is still there against the same dramatic backdrop of Ama Dablam and Everest. But you see it now with the knowledge of what lies beyond it — the glaciers, the high camps, the thin air you have breathed and survived. Rest well tonight. The trail home continues tomorrow.

12

Tengboche to Namche Bazaar

Namche Bazaar · 3,440m · 5 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Back through the forest trail to Namche Bazaar. Your legs are stronger than they were on the way up. Your breathing is easier. You have been to 5,364m and walked home from it — and your body knows it.

Namche Bazaar welcomes you back like an old friend. There is a bakery here that many returning trekkers have mentioned by name in their reviews. You will know the one when you see it. It has apple pie and real coffee and a warm interior that feels like the greatest luxury after days at altitude.

Dinner in Namche tonight takes longer than usual because the stories are coming out. The group that has been building quietly for 12 days finds its voice around the dinner table. Guides and trekkers together, talking and laughing. This is one of the things a small group of 12 achieves that a group of 20 never quite manages.

13

Namche Bazaar to Lukla

Lukla · 2,840m · 6 Hours · Teahouse · All Meals

Your final full day of trekking.

The trail back to Lukla is familiar now — the same forests, the same suspension bridges, the same river rushing below. But you are walking it as a completely different person than the one who walked it in the other direction less than two weeks ago. Your legs are stronger. Your breathing is easier. You have been to 5,364 meters and Kala Patthar at 5,643 meters and walked home from both.

Lukla arrives in the afternoon. Your guide arranges the celebratory dinner — the real one, with your porter and guide at the table as equals, not as staff. There are toasts. There are photographs. Your guide gives you your trek completion certificate.

The porter who has carried your bag for nearly two weeks is a person you know now. You know their name, what they ordered for breakfast most mornings, and something about their family. Tipping tonight is not a transaction. It is a thank you from one human to another. Sleep comes quickly. Your body has walked over 130 kilometers at altitude and it is ready to rest.

14

Fly Lukla to Kathmandu

Kathmandu · 1,350m · 40 Min Flight · Hotel · Breakfast

The morning flight from Lukla back to Kathmandu is subject to mountain weather — visibility must be clear for pilots to navigate the approach. Most mornings in trekking season the flights operate on schedule. Occasionally there are delays of a few hours. Your guide monitors the flight status and keeps you informed throughout.

The 40-minute flight that took you to a different world nearly two weeks ago now takes you back to it. Kathmandu from the air looks vast and chaotic after days of mountain villages and glacial valleys.

Your hotel room. A hot shower with real water pressure. A mirror that shows you a face that has been somewhere and done something. Most trekkers stand in the shower for longer than usual on this day.

The afternoon is yours — Thamel for souvenir shopping, a coffee at a rooftop cafe overlooking the city, or simply a rest that feels genuinely earned. Optional group farewell dinner for anyone who wants one more evening together before scattering back to different corners of the world.

15

International Departure

Kathmandu · 1,350m · Airport Transfer · Breakfast

Final breakfast at the hotel. Our representative will transfer you to Tribhuvan International Airport in time for your onward flight home.

You leave Nepal lighter in your bag and heavier in your memory than when you arrived. You stood at 5,364 meters at Everest Base Camp. You stood at 5,643 meters on Kala Patthar and watched the sun rise over the world's tallest mountain. You walked over 130 kilometers at altitude. You shared 15 days with a small group of people from different corners of the world, guided by people who were born in the valley you trekked through.

You came to check something off a list. You leave understanding why people who do this come back and do it again.

Come back. Nepal is not finished with you yet.

YOUR SAFETY ON THE TREK

Altitude Sickness Is Real.
Here Is Exactly How We Handle It.

We are not going to tell you that altitude sickness is rare or that you almost certainly will not experience it. That would be dishonest.

What we will tell you is this: altitude sickness affects people regardless of age, fitness level, or prior trekking experience. There is no reliable way to predict who will be affected or how severely. What there IS a reliable way to do is catch it early, respond correctly, and make smart decisions — and that is precisely what our 20 years of guiding experience and our daily monitoring protocols exist to do.

Here is what we actually do to keep every trekker in our groups safe.

Daily Blood Oxygen Monitoring — Every Trekker, Every Day

Your guide carries a pulse oximeter throughout the entire trek. Every morning before you start walking and every evening when you arrive at your teahouse, your blood oxygen saturation — your SpO2 — is recorded.

At sea level, a healthy SpO2 reading is between 95% and 100%. At altitude, readings drop. Your guide knows the thresholds at each altitude point that indicate normal adjustment versus early warning signs of altitude sickness.

If your reading drops to a level that concerns your guide, the response is immediate — adjusted pace, extra rest, increased hydration, and close monitoring. That decision is never delayed, never minimized, and never negotiated.

In 20 years, early detection through daily monitoring has kept our serious altitude sickness incident rate at a fraction of the industry average.

Two Genuine Acclimatization Days Built into Every Departure

Our 15-day itinerary includes two deliberate acclimatization days — one in Namche Bazaar at 3,440 meters on Day 5 and one in Dingboche at 4,410 meters on Day 8.

These are not optional. They are not negotiable. And they are not simply rest days.

On each acclimatization day, you hike to a higher altitude than your overnight stop — following the medically validated principle of "climb high, sleep low." Your body produces additional red blood cells during this process, which improves oxygen delivery and dramatically increases your chances of reaching Base Camp.

The difference between a 14-day and a 15-day EBC itinerary is most often found right here. One extra day makes a measurable difference to Base Camp completion rates. We built our itinerary around the science, not around minimizing the days you need off work.

Emergency Evacuation Planning — Every Step of the Route

At every overnight stop on the EBC route, your guide knows the nearest helicopter landing zone. If emergency evacuation becomes necessary, your guide initiates contact with our Kathmandu office, who coordinates with rescue services on your behalf.

This is why travel insurance covering helicopter evacuation is not optional for our treks. Helicopter evacuation from the Everest region costs between $5,000 and $15,000 USD. Your guide can initiate that evacuation within minutes of a serious incident. Your insurance handles the cost.

In 20 years, we have coordinated emergency evacuations for a small number of trekkers. Every one of those trekkers reached safety quickly and without complications because the system worked exactly as designed.

Professional First Aid on Every Trek

Every Nepal Social Treks guide carries a full professional first aid kit throughout the entire 15-day trek. This includes:

· Pulse oximeter for daily oxygen monitoring
· Gamow bag — a portable hyperbaric chamber for severe altitude sickness cases
· Diamox — altitude sickness medication, administered when appropriate
· Complete wound care supplies
· Pain, fever, and anti-nausea medication
· Emergency splinting equipment

Your guide is trained in wilderness first aid, altitude sickness recognition and response, and emergency evacuation procedures — through recognized certification programs, not a one-day company course.

What happens if I get altitude sickness on the trek? +

Your guide will identify it through daily monitoring before it becomes severe in most cases. If symptoms indicate that descent is necessary, your guide will make that call directly and without delay. Can I take altitude medication like Diamox? +

Diamox — acetazolamide — is a prescription medication that helps the body acclimatize faster. Many EBC trekkers take it as a preventive measure. Whether to take it is a decision to make with your doctor before departure, not on the mountain. Your guide carries Diamox in the group first aid kit for emergency use. We recommend consulting your doctor specifically about high-altitude trekking and Diamox at least 6 weeks before your departure date.

What is the success rate of your trekkers reaching Base Camp? +

The majority of our trekkers reach Everest Base Camp. Honestly — how difficult is the Everest Base Camp Trek? +
Honestly: it is a sustained physical challenge that most reasonably fit people can complete with proper preparation and the right itinerary.

The terrain is non-technical — no climbing, no ice, no roping up. The paths are well-worn and walked by thousands of trekkers every season. The difficulty comes from three things: the daily walking hours (5–7 hours per day), the cumulative fatigue of 12 consecutive trekking days, and the altitude.

Of these three, altitude is the variable that cannot be predicted from fitness alone. A well-prepared amateur and a seasoned marathon runner can experience altitude differently with no correlation to their fitness levels. This is why our acclimatization days and daily oxygen monitoring matter more than any other factor in our itinerary.

On a scale of 1 to 10 where 1 is a day hike and 10 is technical mountaineering: EBC is a 5 for physically active people with proper preparation. It is a 7 for people who have not done sustained multi-day walking before.
What are the teahouses actually like? Give me honest expectations. +
Below 4,000 meters — Namche, Phakding, Tengboche — teahouses are comfortable and relatively well-appointed. Clean rooms, good food variety, sometimes hot showers, often Wi-Fi.

Above 4,000 meters — Dingboche, Lobuche, Gorak Shep — teahouses are more basic. Rooms are small and simple. Heating is limited. Showers may not be available. The food is nutritious and plentiful but the menu becomes more limited.

At Gorak Shep — the highest overnight stop at 5,164 meters — the teahouse is basic by any standard. The beds are narrow, the rooms are cold, and it is one of the best nights of the entire trek because of where you are and what you are about to do.

We select the best available teahouse at every stop. Best available does not mean luxury. It means clean, safe, warm enough, and run by good people. That is what you will find.
What is the weather like on the trek? +
Spring departures (March–May): Mornings are cold at higher altitudes — below freezing above 4,000 meters. Days warm up significantly once the sun is on the trail. Afternoons can bring cloud buildup. Rhododendrons are in bloom on the lower trails in March and April.

Autumn departures (September–November): Generally the clearest and most stable trekking weather of the year. October and early November offer exceptional visibility — the kind that makes every summit view a revelation. Temperatures at altitude are colder than spring. Above 4,000 meters, night temperatures regularly drop below -10°C. Warm layers and a good sleeping bag are non-negotiable.

Both seasons are excellent for EBC trekking. The choice comes down to your schedule, your preference for wildflowers versus crystal-clear summit views, and which departure dates work for your life.

How should I train for this trek? +
We send every booked trekker a complete 3-month training program. The short version:

Months 1–2: Build your base. Weekly long hikes of 3–5 hours. Stair climbing 2–3 times per week. Cardio at sustained moderate intensity — not sprinting, not gym circuits. Long and steady.

Month 3: Add load. Begin hiking with a daypack weighing 5–7kg. Increase long hike duration to 5–7 hours. Practice consecutive days of walking — two or three days in a row without full rest days.

The goal: arrive in Kathmandu able to walk 6 hours on consecutive days without significant pain or fatigue. If you can do that, the physical side of the trek is manageable. The altitude then becomes the main variable — and that is what our itinerary is designed to handle.

OUR STORY

Twenty Years Ago, We Had a Vision.
Today, It Is Your Adventure.

Nepal Social Treks was founded in 2006 by a team of passionate Nepali guides who believed a simple thing: the best people to guide you through the Himalayas are the people who grew up in the Himalayas.

That belief has not changed in twenty years. What has changed is the number of trekkers from across America and around the world who have trusted us to get them to Everest Base Camp and back safely.

We are not a large company. We have never tried to be. We operate small groups on purpose, we maintain direct relationships with every trekker who books with us, and we employ a core team of guides and porters who have been with us for years — people whose welfare, wages, and working conditions are our direct responsibility, not an outsourced concern.

In 2015, the earthquake that devastated parts of Nepal affected communities across the Khumbu region. Nepal Social Treks suspended trekking operations during the immediate recovery period and redirected resources to the villages we had spent years building relationships with. We came back to full operations only when the trails were safe and the communities were ready. That decision cost us a season of revenue and we would make it again without hesitation.

Through Covid, we kept our guide and porter team employed as long as we possibly could before the extended closure made it impossible. When trekking resumed, every one of our core guides came back. That tells you something about how we treat our people.

We are 100% locally owned. When you book with Nepal Social Treks, your money does not flow to a corporate headquarters in another country. It pays the wages of Nepali people who love these mountains and the guides who will walk every step with you.

We are proud of what we have built over twenty years. We are prouder of the people who built it with us.

Thank you for considering us for what we genuinely believe is one of the great adventures available to any person on earth.

— The Nepal Social Treks Team, Kathmandu
